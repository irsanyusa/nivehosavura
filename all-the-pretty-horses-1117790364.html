All the Pretty Horses

Publish date: 2024-09-10

Bestseller adaptations have traditionally provided a solid foundation for studios’ holiday season slates. Cormac McCarthy’s bestseller, “All The Pretty Horses” by director Billy Bob Thornton and screenwriter Ted Tally is not exception. As if it didn’t take long enough for the beloved novel to finally get before cameras, the protracted post-production period has forced distributor Miramax to repeatedly rescheduled its opening, with a seemingly definitive October opening postponed until Christmas — and even at press time, in early December, the “Pretty Horses” are still not out of the gate for critics to assess.

Such delays tend to brew their own rumors, including one that Thornton could simply not trim his four-hour rough cut down to a releasable length. Yet this, as well as so much else associated with the film, portends significant Oscar play. Let’s not forget the “Titanic” rumor mill and the sense that it was the epic James Cameron would never finish.

McCarthy’s novel is this year’s “The English Patient” and “The Talented Mr. Ripley” (also starring Matt Damon) and “The Cider House Rules” wrapped into one, books with extraordinarily passionate followings which, against hefty odds, played exceptionally well with the Acad.

Tally is the man who forged best picture winner “The Silence of the Lambs” (1991) from novel to script. One of the rare American writers who’s prominent both as a playwright and screenwriter, he has received the National Board of Review’s award for best screenplay this year. Damon’s presence as star has its own Oscar cachet: Though the thesp has won only for his “Good Will Hunting” (1997) script, he tends to be associated with multinominated films. And Thornton’s star rose on the Oscar attention to his 1996 debut indie feature, “Sling Blade.”

Observers wonder, however, about the ability of Thornton — accustomed to the intimate scale of “Sling” and his still-unreleased “Daddy & Them” — to negotiate the swamps of a big, sprawling Hollywood Western costing enough that Miramax and Sony shared the expenses, and the well-publicized post-production delays tend to sustain the doubts.

Fans of the novel have expressed their own worries about the casting of Damon as naive yet tough vagabonding cowpoke John Grady Cole, a role that was almost Leonardo DiCaprio’s and then Brad Pitt’s.

Though the trailer suggests a grand entertainment in the George Stevens tradition, resemblances to the forgettable 1998 “The Hi-Lo Country,” also a Western co-starring Penelope Cruz released at Christmas, are there for anyone to see. For all that, McCarthy fans, a hardy bunch, remain hopeful.
